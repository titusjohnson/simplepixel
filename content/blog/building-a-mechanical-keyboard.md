There's something deeply satisfying about typing on a mechanical keyboard. The tactile feedback, the audible click, the sense that each keystroke matters. After years of using off-the-shelf keyboards, I decided it was time to build my own from scratch. What followed was a journey into the rabbit hole of switches, keycaps, PCBs, and the surprisingly passionate community of keyboard enthusiasts.

![A collection of mechanical keyboard switches in various colors](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1618384887929-16ec33fab9ef?w=800)

## Why Build Your Own?

The question I get most often is simple: why bother? You can buy excellent mechanical keyboards from companies like Keychron, Ducky, or even mainstream brands like Logitech. The answer, for me at least, comes down to customization and understanding.

When you build your own keyboard, you control every aspect of the experience. You choose the switches that feel right under your fingers. You pick keycaps that match your aesthetic. You can tune the acoustics, adjust the typing angle, and create something that's uniquely yours. It's the difference between buying a suit off the rack and having one tailored specifically for you.

But beyond the practical benefits, there's the satisfaction of understanding how something works. When I type on my custom keyboard now, I know exactly what's happening with each keystroke. I soldered every switch. I programmed every layer. That knowledge creates a connection to the tool that's hard to replicate any other way.

## Choosing the Components

The first step was research. Lots of research. The mechanical keyboard community has developed an incredible depth of knowledge about every aspect of keyboard design, and I spent weeks reading forums, watching videos, and absorbing as much as I could.

### The Case

I went with an aluminum case in a deep navy blue. The weight and rigidity of metal cases provide better acoustics and a more premium feel than plastic alternatives. The case I chose has a 7-degree typing angle, which felt most comfortable during my testing. Some builders prefer gasket-mounted designs that allow the plate to flex slightly, but I opted for a more traditional top-mount construction for this first build.

### The Switches

This is where things get controversial. The keyboard community has strong opinions about switches, and for good reason. The switch is the heart of the typing experience. After testing dozens of options, I settled on tactile switches with a 62-gram actuation force. They provide noticeable feedback without being too heavy for extended typing sessions.

![Close-up of a keyboard PCB with switches being installed](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1595225476474-87563907a212?w=800)

The switch market has exploded in recent years. Beyond the classic Cherry MX lineup, manufacturers like Gateron, Kailh, and countless boutique makers produce switches with every possible characteristic. Linear switches glide smoothly from top to bottom. Tactile switches have a bump partway through the keystroke. Clicky switches add an audible click to that bump. Within each category, you can find options ranging from featherlight 35-gram springs to finger-workout 78-gram monsters.

### The Keycaps

Keycaps might seem like a purely aesthetic choice, but they significantly affect the typing feel and sound. I chose a set of double-shot PBT keycaps in a retro colorway that reminded me of old computer terminals. PBT plastic is more durable and resistant to shine than the more common ABS, though it typically costs more.

The profile matters too. SA keycaps are tall and sculpted, reminiscent of vintage typewriters. Cherry profile sits lower and is what most people picture when they think of mechanical keyboards. DSA and XDA profiles are uniform across all rows, making them versatile but less ergonomic for touch typists. I went with Cherry profile for its familiarity and comfort.

## The Build Process

With all components in hand, the actual assembly took about four hours. The most time-consuming part was lubing the switches. This optional step involves applying a thin layer of lubricant to each switch's moving parts to reduce friction and improve smoothness. It's tedious work, but the difference is noticeable.

Soldering was more straightforward than I expected. Modern keyboard PCBs are well-designed, with clearly marked pads and plenty of space to work. I used a temperature-controlled iron set to 350 degrees Celsius and worked methodically through each switch. The key is patience. Rushing leads to cold joints and frustration.

![Workbench with soldering equipment and keyboard parts](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517077304055-6e89abbf09b0?w=800)

After soldering, I installed the stabilizers for the larger keys like spacebar, shift, and enter. These prevent wobble and ensure consistent keypresses across the entire key surface. I used screw-in stabilizers that I had pre-lubed with a heavier grease than I used for the switches.

The final step was programming. Most custom keyboards use QMK or VIA firmware, which allows incredible customization. I set up multiple layers for different tasks. My base layer is standard QWERTY, but I have a layer for navigation that turns HJKL into arrow keys like Vim, a layer for function keys, and a layer for media controls. The programmability alone justifies building custom for many enthusiasts.

## Was It Worth It?

Absolutely. The total cost came to around $300, which is more than many pre-built options but less than some high-end commercial boards. More importantly, I ended up with exactly the keyboard I wanted, not a compromise.

![Finished mechanical keyboard on a desk setup](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1541140532154-b024d705b90a?w=800)

The typing experience is sublime. The switches have exactly the tactility I was looking for. The keycaps feel substantial under my fingers. The sound is a satisfying thock rather than a harsh clack. And when friends ask about my keyboard, I can explain not just what it is, but how and why I built it.

This first build won't be my last. I'm already planning a second keyboard with linear switches for gaming, and I've been eyeing some interesting split keyboard designs that might help with ergonomics. The hobby has a way of drawing you deeper, and I'm happy to follow where it leads.

If you're considering building your own keyboard, my advice is simple: just start. The community is welcoming, the resources are abundant, and the result is something you'll use every day for years to come. There's no better tool than one you've made yourself.
